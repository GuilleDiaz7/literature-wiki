# La estatua

Original German title and original publication date unconfirmed in sources. [date uncertain — verify] By [Günter Grass](../authors/grass_gunter.md), Nobel Prize in Literature 1999.

## Synopsis

A miniature homage to paper and imagination. The narrative begins with Grass and his wife receiving an invitation to cross the Berlin Wall to read from their books; proceeds through imaginary dinners with medieval figures and the founding fathers of Germany; then pursues the model who posed for the statue of Uta von Naumburg — considered by Umberto Eco the most fascinating woman of the Middle Ages — through a cascade of leaps between reality, history, fantasy, and story. The text consistently withholds whether Grass is declaring love to the faculties of literature, recording late-life eroticism, or encoding a concrete passion for a real woman. (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-02-05_la-estatua-grass.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-02-05_la-estatua-grass.md) frames *La estatua* as Grass's valediction to literature — a miniature that pays homage to paper as the space "where everything is possible." Torné's central critical observation is that the book's most interesting quality is Grass's skill in withholding: behind a "cascade of coloured literary veils," the text encodes a message it refuses to decode. The cascade — reality → history → fantasy → story-imagination — blurs whether the book is a love letter to literature, a record of late-life eroticism, or purgation of a real passion.

Torné detects coded remorse directed at Grass's wife Ute, noting the Uta/Ute parallel (the statue's name and his wife's name). He compares the narrator to the governess of *The Turn of the Screw* — an innocent unable to see clearly — though he notes Grass is "anything but a Victorian innocent." He closes by inviting readers into "this apparatous and fascinating labyrinth in miniature."
