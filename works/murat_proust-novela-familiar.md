# Proust, novela familiar

## Bibliographic Summary

Spanish edition: *Proust, novela familiar*. Trans. María Teresa Gallego and Amaya García Gallego. Anagrama, 288 pp., €19.90. Original French title: *Proust, roman familial* (2023). [date uncertain — verify] By [Laure Murat](../authors/murat_laure.md), French historian and writer, repudiated daughter of an aristocratic family whose surnames appear in Proust's *Recherche*.

## Synopsis

Murat uses Marcel Proust to narrate the history of her own family — a collection of memories that examine the psychology and morality of a class that has lost political and economic power but continues to fascinate in life and fiction. The book's castle has a presence in the narrative. The nucleus is Murat's series of vital decisions (working, being feminist, loving another woman) that made her unassumable for her family. It is also an invitation to read Proust and a personal testament: "Proust saved my life." (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-05-12_proust-novela-familiar-murat.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-05-12_proust-novela-familiar-murat.md) identifies Murat's approach as "particularly original and suggestive": using Proust not to comment on literature but as a lens for her own family history. The danger — falling into the same aristocratic emptiness the book critiques (a display of names and dates) — is navigated through vivid portraits of her father and mother and "precise and penetrating" prose passages.

Torné praises the serene, non-dramatic tone in which Murat recounts her exclusion from her family: she brings to this material the emotional containment she learned from the aristocracy itself, but adds "touches of deep understanding and tenderness that give the measure of the woman she has become by her own merits." He closes by endorsing Murat's central claim — "Proust saved my life" — as genuinely convincing rather than sentimental. He characterises Proust as the most "punk" writer (dissolving all social distinction) and the most "cruel" (at the service of our lucidity).
