# El Vado de los Zorros

## Bibliographic Summary

Spanish edition: *El Vado de los Zorros*. Trans. Viktoria Leftérova and Enrique Maldonado. Impedimenta, 776 pp., €34.95. Original Russian title and year unconfirmed in sources. [date uncertain — verify] By [Anna Starobinets](../authors/starobinets_anna.md), nicknamed "the Russian queen of horror."

## Synopsis

Set in a remote zone of China during the Second World War. The guide through this landscape is Maxim Cronin, a circus artist who has escaped from a concentration camp. The novel is not concerned with military or social history but with a crossing of mystical and marvellous elements: fox-women, mysterious monks, perverse gods, werewolves, mad scientists seeking the ultimate weapon. The novel pumps strange and dark novelties across 700 pages, employing diverse narrative techniques in succession. (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-11-12_el-vado-zorros-starobinets.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-11-12_el-vado-zorros-starobinets.md) opens by contesting the "magical realism" label often applied to Starobinets. His definitional argument: magical realism requires a realistic framework as dominant mode; Starobinets's work places the fantastic at the heart of the narrative from the outset (as do Shelley, Poe, King) — and thus does not qualify. The correct category is "exotic and eccentric historicism": exotic because set in a remote China, eccentric because the interest is not historical but mystical and marvellous.

Torné characterises the novel's dominant poetics as *apabullamiento* — overwhelming — and finds its closest comparandum in Thomas Pynchon: "a Pynchon who takes his characters' eccentricities quite seriously," though without Pynchon's wide-ranging critique of market narratives and military capitalism. The review ends without resolving the enigma of Maxim's fate, leaving the question open.
