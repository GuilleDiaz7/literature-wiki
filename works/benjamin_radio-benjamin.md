## Synopsis

A series of radio broadcasts Benjamin made for children ("the youngest ones") around 1930, fascinated with radio as a pedagogical — and potentially propagandistic — tool. The broadcasts adopt a fluid form halfway between chronicle and story: informative, suggestive, with a tone of surprise and adventure (witches, earthquakes, highwaymen). Benjamin simplifies his syntax and abandons erudite exquisiteness but never adapts topics to an Adamic vision of childhood, nor hastens narratives toward moral conclusions. He treats children as "a less trained species of adults who deserve respect." (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-10-01_radio-benjamin.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-10-01_radio-benjamin.md) evaluates the edition in two parts. On the broadcasts: they deploy the storyteller's art Benjamin theorised elsewhere; they filter "always-suggestive reflections on moral questions (evil, delinquency, repression, catastrophe) interwoven with politics and social dynamics." On Judy Kaufmann's illustrations: they successfully translate Benjamin's philosophical universe into a visual language, using iconic silhouettes as idea-condensations and page-level compositions (mosaics, architectural structures, dreamscapes) that mirror Benjamin's own intellectual tics: "reticence to linear argumentation, distrust of conclusions, taste for the aphorism, the parable, and the enigmatic fulguration."

Torné's central observation: Kaufmann's visual poetics is not merely illustrative but structurally homologous to Benjamin's prose thinking — "closer the stranger it is."
