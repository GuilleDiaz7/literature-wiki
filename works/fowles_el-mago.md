# El Mago

## Bibliographic Summary

Spanish edition: *El Mago*. Trans. Enrique Murillo. Anagrama, 680 pp., €26.90. Original English title: *The Magus* (1966; revised edition 1977). By [John Fowles](../authors/fowles_john.md). This is a reissue reviewed in late 2025. The novel has been in print continuously and is considered a cult classic.

## Synopsis

A harsh Oxford orphan takes a teaching post on a Greek island and falls into the psychological games and maquinations of an "eccentric millionaire" who may be a magician, a madman, or a fraud. The protagonist is variously victim and beneficiary of elaborate scenarios that blur reality, fantasy, and theatrical manipulation. The island setting, the labyrinthine intrigue, and the question of whether what is seen is truth or madness sustain the novel's 680 pages. (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-12-31_el-mago-fowles.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-12-31_el-mago-fowles.md) situates *El Mago* firmly on the readers' side of the reader-critic divide: a classic that belongs more to readers than critics. He notes critical objections — rough narrative voice, mechanical dialogues, imperfectly integrated erudite material, artificial intrigue that loses interest on rereading — while conceding these have not diminished its enormous readership.

The review's two intellectual contributions: (1) a critique of the "postmodern" label, arguing it applies chronologically but not philosophically, since perspectivism and the situatedness of all character utterances is not postmodern novelty but the essential condition of the novel since Cervantes; (2) a reading of the "figure of negative charisma" — the eccentric millionaire as the will that presents itself as superior, inviting surrender of freedom for security. This places *El Mago* in the tradition of Thomas Mann's *Doktor Faustus* and Iris Murdoch's novels of charismatic domination.

## Interpretive Controversies

### Controversy: Is *El Mago* a postmodern novel?

- **Position A** ([Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md)): Chronologically yes (post-Joyce/Woolf modernism), but philosophically no: the novel's perspectivism is not postmodern innovation but the essential condition of all fiction since Cervantes's *Quixote*.
- **Position B**: [Scholars working within postmodern literary theory could defend the label on grounds of metafiction, self-referentiality, and the novel's multiple drafted endings — none of which Torné addresses.]
- **Status**: Torné's position stated; no counter-reading yet in the wiki.

### Controversy: Is *El Mago* a masterwork?

- **Position A** ([Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md)): "I have serious doubts that *El Mago* is a masterpiece, but I am certain that if you enter its game you will have excellent times."
- **Position B**: [The novel's large readership implies a de facto popular canonisation; critical opinion is divided between cult appreciation and formal dismissal.]
- **Status**: Torné's position is candid and productive — neither dismissal nor uncritical praise.
