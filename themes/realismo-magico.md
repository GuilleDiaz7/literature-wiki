# Realismo mágico (Magical Realism)

## Definition

Magical realism is a literary mode in which fantastic or supernatural elements are presented as matter-of-fact within an otherwise realistic narrative world. Associated most prominently with Latin American fiction (García Márquez, Borges, Rulfo) but present in European and world literature more broadly (Kafka, Grass, Grass, Bulgakov, Woolf). Within this wiki, the term appears as an analogy, not a genre classification: [Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) invokes magical realism cautiously ("ya me perdonarán" — "forgive me") to characterise the *type* of imagination at work in Gospodínov, not to classify him as a magical realist author per se.

## Critical Treatments

- [Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) applies the term twice, in opposing ways:
  - For Gospodínov: as a cautious analogy ("ya me perdonarán") for a *type* of imagination — not a genre classification.
  - For Starobinets: as a label he explicitly contests. His argument is definitional: magical realism requires a dominant realistic framework into which the fantastic intrudes. Starobinets places the fantastic at the narrative's heart from the outset — as do Shelley, Poe, Stoker, and King. To include these writers in "magical realism" would be nonsensical; therefore Starobinets does not qualify. The correct category for *El Vado de los Zorros* is "exotic and eccentric historicism."

> [!contradiction] The two uses of the magical realism label in this wiki are incompatible: Torné uses it analogically for Gospodínov and rejects it definitionally for Starobinets. The definitional argument (Starobinets) clarifies what the analogy (Gospodínov) left vague. Taken together, they imply: Gospodínov's imagination has an *affinity* with magical realism's type of inventiveness without placing the fantastic at the narrative's core; Starobinets's work is structurally fantastic throughout and therefore categorically distinct.
