# Las intermitencias del corazón (The Heart's Intermittencies)

## Definition

"Las intermitencias del corazón" (the heart's intermittencies) is a concept used by [Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) (see [source](../sources/2026-02-09_amores-inconstantes-constant.md)) to describe a tradition of French literature built on the premise — credited to Blaise Pascal — that "the heart has reasons reason does not know" (*le cœur a ses raisons que la raison ne connaît point*).

In literary terms, this tradition concerns the systematic study of the discrepancies between what is rational, convenient, or dignified for a person to want — and what the hidden motives of the heart actually drive them to pursue. The tradition presupposes that erotic and emotional life operates according to a logic opaque even to the subject experiencing it, and that this opacity is the central subject of a certain strand of French fiction.

Torné uses the term as a framework for reading [Benjamin Constant's *nouvelles*](../works/constant_amores-inconstantes.md): the *amores inconstantes* are a study not of grand passion but of the secret grammar of the heart at odds with itself.

## Critical Treatments

- [Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) proposes this tradition as the proper interpretive frame for Constant — as an alternative to the biographical reading. He traces the tradition: Pascal → Constant → Balzac → Proust.
- Balzac's contribution: the social fresco; vast networks of characters; the heart mapped in its social ecology.
- Proust's contribution: temporal depth; the pursuit and mutation of a feeling over decades.
- Constant's contribution: radical concentration; the aphoristic study of one couple (or two) stripped of social panorama and temporal accumulation. Chamber music.
