# Sátira social (Social Satire)

## Definition

Social satire uses irony, exaggeration, and comic defamiliarization to expose the absurdities, hypocrisies, and injustices of social norms and institutions. The tradition runs from Aristophanes through Swift, Voltaire, Austen, Dickens, and into contemporary fiction. In this wiki, the term covers works that use comic or ironic modes as instruments of cultural critique, whether the target is body-cult, wellness culture, class pretension, political corruption, or literary convention itself.

A key critical question tracked in the wiki: the difference between satire as instrument (the comedy serves a larger moral or political argument) vs. satire as entertainment (comic pleasure as the primary end). Torné tends to ask whether a satirical work "needs" its specific target or whether a more general comic competence is at work.

## Critical Treatments

- [Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md): distinguishes between satire that serves a larger moral argument (Shriver at her best, e.g. *Kevin*) and satire that remains sociological surface comedy. The body-cult novel is competent but lacks the psychological depth of Shriver's peak work.
