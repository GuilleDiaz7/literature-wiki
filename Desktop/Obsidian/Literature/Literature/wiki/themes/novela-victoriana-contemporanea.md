# Novela victoriana contemporánea (Contemporary Victorian Novel)

## Definition

A recognisable sub-genre of contemporary British fiction in which writers set one or more novels in the Victorian period — not to produce historical fiction in the conventional sense, but to engage with, measure themselves against, or play within the age of the English novel's greatest vitality. [Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) frames this as "almost a rite of passage" for contemporary British writers.

The defining tension of the sub-genre: how do contemporary techniques, perspectives, and purposes inscribe themselves within the Victorian material without producing mere pastiche? The challenge is to bring the present into the past (gender, race, class, sexuality that Victorians could not address openly) while honouring the structural and moral weight of the tradition being invoked.

The genre's prestige models are invoked in contrast to its weaker examples: the Victorian country-house novel as a literary achievement (Austen, Dickens, Forster, Murdoch) versus the same setting deployed decoratively (cf. [*Albión*](../works/hope_albion.md)).

## Critical Treatments

- [Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) identifies the sub-genre and its key examples: Ian McEwan (*Expiación*), Julian Barnes (*Arthur y George*), Kazuo Ishiguro (*Nunca me abandones*), Zadie Smith (*La impostura*). The richer the formal and thematic ambition, the further the novel is from mere pastiche.
- Torné's key evaluative distinction: Victorian models (Austen, Dickens, Forster, Murdoch) used rural or urban settings as backdrops for substantive moral and literary investigations. Contemporary Victorian novels succeed when they do likewise; they fail when they use the setting as content.
