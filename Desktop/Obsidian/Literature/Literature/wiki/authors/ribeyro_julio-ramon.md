# Julio Ramón Ribeyro

## Biographical Context

Julio Ramón Ribeyro (Lima, 1929–1994) spent many years in Paris — a trajectory shared with fellow Peruvian poets César Vallejo and César Moro, and with Vargas Llosa (with whom Ribeyro had a "cordially tense" relationship). His Parisian exile was both creative and isolating. He died in 1994.

## Major Works

- [*La tentación del fracaso* (diary, 1950–1978)](../works/ribeyro_tentacion-del-fracaso.md) — reviewed by Serra Bradford
- [*La palabra del mudo* (complete stories)](../works/ribeyro_tentacion-del-fracaso.md) — mentioned in the same review
- *Prosas apátridas* — incassifiable collection; possibly his best self (Serra Bradford)

## Style and Technique

As a diarist: a voice recognizable from first to last day, converting private annotations into literary art. His genius lies in *Prosas apátridas* and the short stories; the diary, while significant, is a "genre phantom" — the mold for the true work not quite realized. Obsessive regarding craft: techniques for each kind of problem; a biógrafo describes him as "obsessive of techniques." Paradox: both his diary and his stories "surpass" him as he conceived himself.

## Critical Reputation

Celebrated in Peru and increasingly internationally. [Serra Bradford](../critics/serra-bradford_matias.md) places him in the tradition of diarists: Kafka, Woolf, Jünger. He is less known outside Spanish-speaking literary circles than his quality warrants.
