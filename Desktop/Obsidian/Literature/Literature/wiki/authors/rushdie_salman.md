# Salman Rushdie

## Biographical Context

Salman Rushdie (Bombay, 1947) is one of the most prominent postcolonial novelists in English. His 1988 novel *The Satanic Verses* led to a fatwa issued by Ayatollah Khomeini; Rushdie lived in hiding for years. In 2022 he was stabbed at a public event, losing sight in one eye. *Ciudad Victoria* (*Victory City*, 2023) is his first novel published after the attack.

## Major Works

- *Midnight's Children* (1981) — Booker Prize; not reviewed in this wiki
- *The Satanic Verses* (1988) — fatwa; not reviewed
- [*Ciudad Victoria* (2023)](../works/rushdie_ciudad-victoria.md) — reviewed by Torné

## Style and Technique

Rushdie at his best is in the role of Sheherazade: the gluttonous pleasure of narration. He bastardizes epic material with novelistic resources — intrigue, psychological density, modern irony. The project of rewriting an epic is structurally impossible (too much narrative knowledge has accumulated to "un-learn"), but Rushdie knows this and works around it. His influence: closer to García Márquez than to Hindu epic tradition.

## Critical Reputation

Universally known; reputation complicated by the fatwa and its aftermath. [Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) rates *Ciudad Victoria* as enjoyable — Rushdie at his best in storytelling mode — but notes the political allegorical readings as limited and the universal pessimism as the novel's most serious claim.
