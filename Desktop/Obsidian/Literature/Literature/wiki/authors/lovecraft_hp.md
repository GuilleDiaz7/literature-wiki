# H. P. Lovecraft

## Biographical Context

Howard Phillips Lovecraft (Providence, Rhode Island, 1890–1937) was a "patrician spirit without blazon or patrimony": he published his entire life in low-prestige magazines, without the slightest literary recognition. His life was somewhat sideways and anonymous. His character combined deep racial prejudice with extraordinary intellectual discipline and a serious literary vocation that he pursued in conditions of obscurity.

## Style and Technique

Lovecraft hated sentimental, cheap, and popular art. He dedicated his life to extracting, very slowly, stories devoted to "lo extraño" (the strange): the representation of pressures the unknown exerts upon human nerves. He considered strangeness a primary experience of consciousness, comparable to love or ambition. He also had the tic of telegraphing coming horrors in advance — a narrative mannerism Torné finds undermining. His imagination is vast (almost lysergic temporal scales; highly suggestive images) but his prose is opaque to psychological complexity except fright. His greatest literary power: tireless invention of "strange" situations.

**Paradox of influence**: Lovecraft's work is weak enough, and his influence large enough, that any disciple can write a Lovecraft story better than the master — a backhanded compliment Torné genuinely intends as such.

## Critical Reputation

Converted into a kind of pop idol of horror (cefalópodos de peluche); his influence is beyond calculation. The letters reveal an artist who is the opposite of what this popular reputation suggests: conscientious, rational, self-aware. His bridge function between Gothic terror and science fiction is historically significant.

## Scholarly Controversies

The central controversy flagged but deferred: Volume III (political opinions) will force the question of separating author from work. Lovecraft's racism and misogyny were profound and documented.
