# Andar

## Bibliographic Summary

Spanish edition: *Andar*. Trans. Virginia Maza. Contraseña, 120 pp., €16. Original German title: *Gehen* (1971). By [Thomas Bernhard](../authors/bernhard_thomas.md). The review situates this work within Bernhard's career: transitional between his three major masterworks (*Trastorno*, *La calera*, *Corrección*) and his best chamber works (*El malogrado*, *Maestros antiguos*).

Note: The review credits [Miguel Sáenz](../translators/saenz_miguel.md) as Bernhard's "most constant" Spanish translator — but this edition is translated by Virginia Maza. Sáenz is discussed as a broader cultural phenomenon, not as the translator of this particular volume.

## Synopsis

Two friends walk and recall a third who has been admitted to a sanatorium after a nervous crisis while buying trousers. Bernhard proposes a superficial similarity between walking and thinking; the text demonstrates that thinking is infinitely more monstrous. The prose is a sadistic echo chamber where voices reflect others' words (often memories of a third party), sensitive to paranoid nuances applied to banal subjects, absurd parallelisms, sudden pronouncements, and games with prepositions and italicised emphases. (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-06-28_andar-bernhard.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-06-28_andar-bernhard.md) situates *Andar* precisely within Bernhard's career: post-masterworks (*Trastorno*, *La calera*, *Corrección*), pre-chamber works (*El malogrado*, *Maestros antiguos*). The argument-fit is comic-strip simple; the formal interest lies in Bernhard's "sadistic device" — an echo chamber of voices reflecting other voices, with paranoid nuances, preposition games, and italicised emphases that leave the reader bewildered. This bewilderment is the intended state.

From this bewilderment Bernhard launches his "favourite subjects": punishing procreation, the world as madhouse, genius against the State, the incompetence of psychiatrists, the impossibility of living anywhere. The book's crucial subject — the monstrousness of everyday thinking, the consciousness that feeds parasitically on our energy — is launched via this accumulated disorientation.

Torné makes a notable secondary claim: the "Bernhard virus" that infected Spanish writers is nearly impossible to imitate without parody, and "perhaps the only writer whose prose genuinely benefited from it was his most constant translator: [Miguel Sáenz](../translators/saenz_miguel.md)."
