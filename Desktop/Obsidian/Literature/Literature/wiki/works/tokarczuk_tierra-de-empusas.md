## Synopsis

Set in a sanatorium in Lower Silesia in 1913. The young Miecysław Wojnicz is confined to recover from tuberculosis, in a world modelled on — and parodying — Thomas Mann's *La montaña mágica*. Women circulate as an almost invisible chorus of observers while men debate pompously. The novel denounces the erasure of women: their impune violence, their indispensable but unacknowledged labour sustaining the sanatorium's idle recuperation. (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-03-26_tierra-empusas-tokarczuk.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-03-26_tierra-empusas-tokarczuk.md) opens by contrasting the novel with Tokarczuk's masterwork *Los errantes*: where that book withheld its organising keys until the last pages, *Tierra de empusas* makes its programme visible from the first. "The enigma gives way to the programme."

The programme is parodic feminist denunciation: Tokarczuk infiltrates Mann's *La montaña mágica* to mock the period's masculinity and denounce women's historical erasure. Torné acknowledges her talents — playful imagination charged with malice, prose plasticity, allegorical air — but notes that Mann himself wrote his sanatorium novel with psychological and social tension against his own class's presuppositions. No equivalent tension appears in Tokarczuk's book; Torné agrees phrase by phrase with the feminist argument yet "the reading does not reach me emotionally."

His sharpest objection: Tokarczuk silences the women *of literature* — the canonical female characters of Flaubert, Balzac, Galdós, Tolstoy, Dostoyevsky, and the female authors Austen, Eliot, the Brontës — while denouncing women's erasure. This "elective erasure" gives the reading "a somewhat opportunistic flavour."

## Interpretive Controversies

### Controversy: Does *Tierra de empusas* practise the erasure it denounces?

- **Position A** ([Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md)): Yes. By playing in Mann's literary territory while silencing the overwhelming literary tradition of female representation — both as characters and as authors — Tokarczuk practises a selective erasure that undermines the comprehensiveness of her feminist claim. The book's "borrados electivos" (elective erasures) are its central critical problem.
