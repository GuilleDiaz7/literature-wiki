## Synopsis

Tony Last, the cuckolded protagonist, inhabits an imposing English country house in collision with the advancing urban, industrial, popular world. His wife conducts an affair; the divorce process requires Tony to simulate adultery at a hotel with a stranger (paying detectives to certify it) to avoid "staining" his wife's reputation. The novel traces the decomposition of a marriage and a class with bare, fast dialogue, no narrative transitions, and no interiority. A child dies; the novel ends desolatingly in South America. (Source: [Torné 2025](../sources/2025-06-04_punado-polvo-waugh.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2025 review](../sources/2025-06-04_punado-polvo-waugh.md) opens on Waugh's characteristic world — the decadent aristocracy encountering an accelerating modernity — and notes that time has widened the gap until some passages read anthropologically today. His formal analysis is the review's core: Waugh's writing at this stage is "decisively refractory to the literary" — it amputes description, psychological examination, and narrative transition, leaving bare dialogue and no ornament. The result resembles the skeleton of a television script. Torné identifies four achievements of this anti-literary style: forensic detachment, the transmission of Waugh's own moral repugnance, aristocratic cynicism coating even dramatic passages (including the child's death), and a tone at "the turbid extreme where humour is irreversibly mixed with bitterness."

The review positions *Un puñado de polvo* as a formal counter-model to the English country house novel — where [*Albión*](../works/hope_albion.md) deploys the same setting decoratively, Waugh drains it of sentiment and confronts it with moral repugnance.
