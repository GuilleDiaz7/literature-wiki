## Synopsis

Three novellas of autobiographical appearance, composing a novel through shared tone, technique, and theme. All three are travel narratives, all feature a younger Galgut observed by an older narrator with curiosity but without memory of motivations, and all are desolating. The first recounts an attempted approach to an attractive young German man; the second follows the narrator's pursuit of an elusive young man across Africa and Switzerland; the third accompanies a self-destructive friend on a recuperation trip to India. (Source: [Torné 2024](../sources/2024-12-02_habitacion-ajena-galgut.md))

## Critical Overview

[Gonzalo Torné's 2024 review](../sources/2024-12-02_habitacion-ajena-galgut.md) argues the three novellas form a novel through shared formal and thematic properties. Galgut's style is analytical with lyrical crests in descriptions, sensitised to social differences, with attention to the moral profile traced by each story's impact on the autobiographical narrator.

Torné evaluates each novella separately: the first is predictable and cliché-ridden in its treatment of amorous obsession (Musil-adjacent, but lacking Musil's precision); the second — a fantasy about suspended lives and the danger of hunches — is the book's best; the third collapses into "melodramatic excess of effective disasters" and false crocodile tears. Overall, the book's true subject is displacement rather than travel: "being out of place, protagonising a life we don't recognise as our own."
