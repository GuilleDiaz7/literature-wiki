## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Hombres fatales', de Elisenda Julibert: demolición de un mito." *El Periódico*, 2 November 2022.

Reviewed work: Elisenda Julibert, *Hombres fatales*. [Publisher not specified in source.]

## Summary

The book demolishes the literary myth of the *femme fatale* — the figure on which so many paintings, novels, and films have been built. Julibert's argument: the fatal woman is the invention of an ensimismado, alienated individual for whom a simple disappointment becomes a universal tragedy — "an entrenched conception of love for which alienation is an unmistakable sign of affection." What makes the demolition most interesting is its quality: the pleasure of watching fictional men crumble as their women's supposed malevolence evaporates. Julibert avoids the social-justice framework (legitimate but often mechanical and predictable) in favor of a prolonged examination of the emotions at play — passion, love, jealousy, possession, delirium, alienation, domination — placing herself in the orbit of René Girard, Roland Barthes, and Clément Rosset. Case studies include *Carmen*, *Ese oscuro objeto de deseo*, *Lolita*, *Vértigo*, *Bouvard y Pécuchet*, *Con faldas y a lo loco*. The essays are lucid, precise, avoiding jargon; the prose is sensitive to movements of thought and tends to concentrate in elegant aphorisms. There is a visible progression from Mérimée's naive/shameless presentation of the *femme fatale* to Buñuel's more complex and critical version. The final essays are more propositional: a refutation of Freud's reduction of desire to sexual instinct, and a commentary on *Bouvard et Pécuchet* as defense of "slow love." Torné: "If *Hombres fatales* is not one of the essays of the year, we are in a golden literary course."

## Key Claims

- The *femme fatale* is a toxic masculine invention: the projecting of alienation onto a woman, making her responsible for male suffering.
- Julibert's method is emotional-analytical (Girard, Barthes, Rosset) rather than socio-ideological.
- The book progresses from Mérimée to Buñuel: naive myth → complex critique.
- Final essays propose "slow love" as corrective and refute Freud's reduction of desire.
- Torné considers it one of the year's best essays.
