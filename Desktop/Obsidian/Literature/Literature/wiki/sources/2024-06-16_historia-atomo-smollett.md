# Source: Torné, "Historia y aventuras de un átomo, de Smollett" (2024)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Historia y aventuras de un átomo', de Tobias Smollett: Una vía por desarrollar." *El Periódico*, 16 June 2024.

Reviewed work: Tobias Smollett, *Historia y aventuras de un átomo*. Trans. Victoria León. El Paseo, 192 pp. 22,95 €.

## Summary

Torné opens with his recurring thesis about the novel form: despite decades of death-announcement, the novel's history reveals several developmental possibilities that were aborted before demonstrating their full potential — victims of the triumphant realism that became the dominant mode (Balzac, Tolstoy). Smollett (Dalquhurn, Scotland, 1721–Livorno, 1771) — whose fame Torné notes is permanently linked to his having translated *Don Quixote* into English — tried one such abandoned possibility in this novel: the prolonged report of a very particular witness to a single listener. The narrator is a talking atom that has been present (in the bodies of kings, advisors, generals, in "not always noble" anatomical zones) at key events during the Seven Years' War (1756–1763). The novel recovers the pre-novelistic historiographical mode: a plausible account of important events that aspires less to chronological data than to penetrating the nerve of events while fascinating with eloquence. Smollett then adds a satirical membrane: the action is displaced to a fantasy Japan at war with China, which allows him to be irreverent, caustic, free, and impertinent without quite reaching the vitriolo of Swift. He has no interest in psychological progress, atmosphere, or dramatically plausible dialogue.

## Key Claims

- *Historia y aventuras* is a "vía por desarrollar" — a form abandoned by the triumph of realism.
- The talking atom as narrator recovers pre-novelistic historiographical modes (eloquent, penetrating, not chronological).
- The fantasy-Japan displacement allows irreverence without direct political risk.
- Smollett is permanently linked to the English translation of *Don Quixote*.
- Not realist: no psychology, no atmosphere, no verisimilar dialogue — and this is the point.
- Translator: Victoria León; publisher: El Paseo.

## Relation to Existing Wiki

First Smollett entry. Connects to Torné's broader argument that realism crowded out valid alternatives — a position implicit in many of his reviews. The "vía por desarrollar" label echoes his appreciation of Fowles's *El Mago* and Bernhard's *Andar* as formal experiments that illuminate the novel's unrealized possibilities.
