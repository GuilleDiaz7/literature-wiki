## Summary

A 16-section essay on friendship — its indefiniteness, its literary representations, and its analogy to a game with self-generated rules. Torné begins with an observation from his editor Santiago Gerchunoff: friendship is never exemplary; we befriend not those who suit us or whom we approve of, but those who interest and attract us. The main literary example is Cervantes's Don Quixote and Sancho Panza: their friendship begins asymmetrically and instrumentally (master/nearly-illiterate servant) but is spent discovering what kind of friendship they have, performed through endless dialogue in varied situations. The friendship becomes unambiguous only at the moment of parting, when Sancho understands what he had. Torné also uses Virginia Woolf's diary portraits of her friends (Forster, Eliot, Keynes) — each visit recombines the relational atoms, the same fundamental curiosity sustaining ever-varying portraits. The essay treats the loss of a friend as de-articulating a world in which we played a protagonist's role, leaving us custodians of branches of language that no longer have an addressee. It concludes with the Barnes-Amis rupture: their friendship was severed by Martin Amis's slight to Barnes's wife Pat Kavanagh; near Amis's death, Barnes received him but had not forgiven him. The scene reads as both a theatrical observance of charity and a recognition of the old friendship's claim. Friendship is like a game that generates its own changing rules.

## Key Claims

- Friendship is not exemplary — not chosen for virtue or utility but for attraction and interest.
- The Quixote-Sancho friendship: discovered through hundreds of pages of dialogue in varied situations; performed, not theorized.
- Woolf's diary: friendship as continuously recombining portrait, sustained by singular curiosity.
- Loss of friend: branches of language without addressee; irreplaceable.
- Barnes-Amis: rupture, near-death diplomacy, unresolved — friendship as game with rules all parties recognize.
- Torné's own friendship model: more comfortable in humor than confession; discontinuous; changed across decades.
