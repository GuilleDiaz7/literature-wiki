# Source: Eco, "The Cult of the Imperfect" (2019)

## Bibliographic Details

Eco, Umberto. "The Cult of the Imperfect." *The Paris Review* (blog), 28 October 2019. Trans. Alastair McEwen. Excerpted from: *On the Shoulders of Giants*. Harvard University Press, 2019. Originally published in Italian by La Nave di Teseo Editore.

This is a primary text by Umberto Eco (Alessandria, 1932–Milan, 2016), not a review by Torné.

## Summary

Eco proposes a theory of the "ramshackle" or "unhinged" artwork as a condition for cult status. He uses *The Count of Monte Cristo* as his primary example: one of the most exciting novels ever written, and one of the most badly written — full of holes, shameless repetition, helpless syntax. Dumas wrote this way for financial reasons (paid per line; serialized novels require recapping). Yet Eco, when invited by Einaudi to translate the novel by slimming its redundancies (saving 25% of words at points), gave up after 100 pages: he realized that the wordiness and slovenliness may be structural, integral to the narrative apparatus. The padding has structural value — like graphite rods in a nuclear reactor, it slows the pace to make expectations more excruciating. *Monte Cristo* is not art; it is *mythopoeia* — myth-making. Mythopoeic works create cult because they allow "imperfections," because their archetypes can be disaggregated, quoted, and recombined independently of the whole. Eco then extends the argument to cult films: to generate cult, a film must already be inherently ramshackle, "magnificently unstable," showing not one central idea but many, without a coherent "philosophy of composition." *Rio Bravo* is a cult film; the "perfect" *Stagecoach* is not. *Casablanca* is the supreme example: made piecemeal (Bergman didn't know until the last day whom she was supposed to love), drawing on all tried-and-tested archetypes, full of psychological implausibilities — yet a cult object precisely because "when all archetypes shamelessly burst in, we plumb Homeric depths." Eco reads *Hamlet* through Eliot: Hamlet is a patchwork of prior tragic material, and its greatness may be precisely in the failed fusion — the character's mysterious psychology is the product of unresolved competing topoi. Two clichés are laughable; a hundred clichés are affecting — because they begin to talk to one another.

## Key Claims

- The ramshackle, unhinged work is a condition for cult status — not a defect.
- *Monte Cristo*'s structural value lies in its padding (delays suspense, prolongs agony).
- *Monte Cristo* is mythopoeia, not art — creating myths rather than aesthetic objects.
- Cult films must be inherently unstable, showing many ideas without a coherent philosophy.
- *Casablanca*: all archetypes at once; the film was made up as it went along. This is its strength.
- *Hamlet*: Eliot reads it as a failed patchwork, and this failure is the source of its enduring mystery.
- Two clichés are laughable; a hundred are affecting — because they begin to talk to each other.

## Quotations of Significance

"Two clichés are laughable. A hundred clichés are affecting — because we become obscurely aware that the clichés are talking to one another and holding a get-together."

"By dint of excess, melodrama and kitsch verge on the sublime, while excess tips over into genius."

## Limitations or Biases

Essay advocates a specific thesis (the "ramshackle cult object") with great rhetorical force; the examples (Monte Cristo, Casablanca, Hamlet) are carefully chosen to support the argument.
