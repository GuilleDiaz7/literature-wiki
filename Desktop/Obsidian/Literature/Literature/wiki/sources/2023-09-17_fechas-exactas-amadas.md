# Source: Torné, "Las fechas exactas, de Amadas" (2023)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Las fechas exactas', de Mario Amadas: una precisión tras otra." *El Periódico*, 17 September 2023.

Reviewed work: Mario Amadas, *Las fechas exactas. Setenta días en Ghana*. Colectivo La Máquina, 210 pp. 14 €.

## Summary

Amadas (Barcelona, 1986) presents the novel as a travel diary: the narrator travels to Ghana to participate in a kind of eco-village, knowing nothing about the country. The opening pages contain reflections on what it means to travel and stay somewhere — Torné notes these as immediately revealing Amadas's unusual prose: unpredictable even on well-worn topics, affectionate, precise, "terrifyingly precise." This precision operates in two registers. First: the recreation of singular moments of experience — not by their exceptionality but by the minuteness of reconstruction (sensations, climate, textures), descriptions so vivid they don't sound like descriptions. Second: a continuous moral and political scrutiny of surrounding activity — the narrator is hypersensitive to incompetence, disloyalty, laziness, and abuse, but his scrutiny is crossed by bursts of good humor and a contagious enthusiasm for life. The book develops in two times/two modes: days enclosed in the eco-village (its deficient labor relations) and free days in Ghanaian cities (recognizing the marks colonialism has left). It ends with a brief, splendid chapter of stocktaking. Torné's recommendation is strong: he urges readers to start with *Las fechas exactas*, or *El día que pase algo* (Colectivo La Máquina, 2021), or the Brooklyn diary (Ril Editores, 2019). Amadas is described as one of the few original protrusions of otherwise predictable Spanish prose — an underground literary adventure, victim of editorial blindness.

## Key Claims

- Amadas's precision is the primary literary quality: dual register (phenomenological recreation + moral scrutiny).
- The book is not merely a travel diary but a document of colonial legacies and labor failure in a globalized project.
- Torné places Amadas as one of the few "original protrusions" of contemporary Spanish fiction.
- The small-press distribution (Colectivo La Máquina) is itself diagnosed as editorial blindness.
