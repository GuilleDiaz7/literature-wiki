# Source: Torné, "Un Occidente secuestrado, de Kundera" (2023)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "Crítica de 'Un Occidente secuestrado', de Milan Kundera: dos advertencias del maestro." *El Periódico*, 13 March 2023.

Reviewed work: Milan Kundera, *Un Occidente secuestrado*. Trans. Maika Lahoz. Tusquets, 88 pp. 17 €.

## Summary

Two texts recovered and republished. Text 1: *La literatura y las pequeñas naciones* (1967 lecture at the Congress of Czechoslovak Writers). Kundera defines the "vandal" — educated, self-satisfied, able to transform his homeland into a desert without history, memory, echo, or beauty — as the greatest enemy of minor cultures. Minor cultures (not tied to powerful states) can only justify their existence by abandoning folkloristic corners and offering works of merit to other countries. In a globalized world, dominant culture tends to devour everything — even Spanish culture risks becoming minor or a satellite of Anglo-Saxon culture if it does not resist the vandalism of its own compatriots. Text 2: *Un Occidente secuestrado* (1983 journal article). Kundera denounces the cultural nullification of what he calls "Central Europe" (Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary): Western Europe denounced the absence of freedoms under Communist rule but forgot that with their forced annexation to Russia, these countries' cultural identity — closer to rationalism and Catholicism than to Slavic sentimentalism and Orthodox religion — had also been violated. Torné notes the temptation to read this text as warning against Russian imperialism, but Kundera cautions not to confuse Ukraine and Belarus (politically subjugated but culturally Russian) with Hungary and Poland (subjugated and culturally Central European). Post-liberation, these countries have now accelerated drifts contrary to Enlightenment principles, approaching extremism and intolerance.

## Key Claims

- Small nations must offer works of universal merit or risk cultural absorption.
- In a globalized world, dominant cultures devour minor ones — including potentially Spanish culture as satellite of Anglo-Saxon.
- "Central Europe" had a distinct cultural identity (rationalism, Catholicism) violated by Soviet annexation.
- Ukraine and Belarus are not the same case as Poland and Hungary — do not conflate.
- Political liberation has not washed out all monsters: post-Soviet Hungary and Poland show extremist drifts.

## Limitations or Biases

Both texts are historical documents (1967, 1983); Torné reads them as still valid, but their specific political arguments about 1983 are now complicated by post-1989 developments.
