# Source: Torné, "Ciudad Victoria, de Rushdie" (2023)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Ciudad Victoria', la primera novela de Salman Rushdie tras el atentado: un reino de este mundo." *El Periódico*, 12 February 2023.

Reviewed work: Salman Rushdie, *Ciudad Victoria* (*Victory City*). Trans. Luis Murillo Fort. Random House, 368 pp. 22,90 €.

## Summary

Rushdie's stated ambition is to write an epic poem competing with the *Bhagavad Gita* — better written. The novel's cosmetic layer of fantasy (closer to García Márquez than to the Ramayana) serves as a vehicle for bastardising and hypertrophying the foundational poem with later novelistic resources: intrigue, psychological density, modern irony, and above all the pleasure of storytelling exercised with a storyteller's gluttony. Torné argues that the project is structurally impossible — we know too much about narration to un-learn it and write a genuine epic — but Rushdie is aware of this and plays Sheherazade rather than a Homeric bard. The novel's political readings (parallels with China, Saudi Arabia, India) Torné dismisses as limited; the novel works best surrendered to as story. The fundamental moral: human societies cannot sustain the principles they are founded on — women are subjugated, dissenters crushed, religions persecuted, war becomes the state's main hobby. This generates a universal pessimism echoing Shakespeare's "politics as a faceless nightmare." Against this determinism, Pampa (who lives almost 300 years and refuses to wield power) is the novel's most alive character: the dignity and limits of a power that refuses to impose itself. A soft power that still belongs to the future.

## Key Claims

- Rushdie plays the role of Sheherazade, not epic bard — the novel's mode is storytelling-as-pleasure.
- The epic ambition is structurally impossible (we know too much narrative); Rushdie knows this and works around it.
- Political allegorical readings (China/India/Saudi) are too narrow; the novel's pessimism is universal.
- Pampa: the novel's central character embodies the dignity and limits of non-coercive power.
- Rushdie's prose is in its element when indulging the pleasure of narration.
- Translator: Luis Murillo Fort; publisher: Random House.
