# Source: Torné, "Diario de sueños, de Lovecraft" (2024)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Diario de sueños', de H. P. Lovecraft: ¿quién sueña al que sueña?" *El Periódico*, 20 May 2024.

Reviewed work: H. P. Lovecraft, *Diario de sueños. Cartas de H. P. Lovecraft, Vol. II.* Trans. Javier Calvo. Aristas Martínez, 256 pp. 27,90 €.

## Summary

A companion review to Torné's earlier piece on the first volume of Lovecraft's letters. Since that review, Torné attempted an immersion in Lovecraft's fiction and found it wanting: opaque to any psychological complexity except fright or panic; without economy in deploying geological and mineralogical knowledge; with the tic of telegraphing terrors in advance. Lovecraft's greatest interest, Torné now concludes, lies in his tireless invention of "strange" situations, the suggestive power of his images (despite the promise of their indescribability), and the vast, almost lysergic temporal scales he commands. His influence has escaped all calculation — so much so that any disciple can write a Lovecraft story better than the master (the weakness of his narration combined with his commercial success creates this paradoxical effect). Volume II collects all the dreams Lovecraft sent to friends: fragmentary oneiric images, nightmares, brief narrative developments (one with a Roman theme), paranoid parasites, formless agents, mind-exchanges. Torné finds these raw images more interesting than their fictional elaborations. Javier Calvo's erudite notes situate each image in the corresponding story. The volume ends with Lovecraft's "cat letters," where the usually grim author reveals himself as lively as a Dickens character. A third volume on Lovecraft's political opinions is promised — and dreaded.

## Key Claims

- Torné attempted to read Lovecraft's fiction after the first letters volume and found it inadequate: no psychology, no economy, no suspense (pre-announcement habit).
- Lovecraft's primary value: invention of "strange" situations and vast temporal scales.
- Paradox: his disciples write his stories better than he did — weakness of narration plus commercial success.
- The dream diary (Volume II) offers raw images more interesting than their fictional elaborations.
- Javier Calvo's apparatus is again praised.
- The upcoming political opinions volume will require confronting the problem of separating author from work.
- Translator: Javier Calvo; publisher: Aristas Martínez.
