# Source: Torné, "Sé mía, de Richard Ford" (2024)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Sé mía', de Richard Ford: Cuidados crepusculares." *El Periódico*, 30 June 2024.

Reviewed work: Richard Ford, *Sé mía* (*Be Mine*). Trans. Damià Alou. Anagrama, 400 pp. 21,90 €.

## Summary

The fifth Frank Bascombe novel. For familiar readers it is a farewell party, a parade of summoned shadows for the last dance. For newcomers, it is self-sufficient. The novel's unusual angle: how does a man approaching eighty assess the happiness of his life? Bascombe has survived two divorces, a dead child, and various professional setbacks. But the American measuring-stick for happiness is the capacity to improve — and time no longer offers that. The narrator shares the American novelist's mania for naming everything: brands, roads, tyres, shops — Torné compares this to Updike's sensual delirium, but notes that Frank never lets an opportunity pass without naming it. His prose is tonal: atone, without large emotional pours, with a very nuanced irony sensitive to American verbal habits. His assessments have the quality of cold stoicism, a disillusioned lucidity without remorse. This tone is responsible for the sharpness of the rare moments of emotivity — particularly a late-love scene with a young masseuse. The plot: Frank insists on road-tripping with his son Paul, sick with ALS, to see Mount Rushmore. Preparations dominate the first half (functional dialogue, past-memory, family reproaches). The second half adopts the road-movie genre, flirting with symbolic dimension (the great mount of American power) resolved ironically. Paul is characterized through his disease's suppression of emotional display — the mystery of him is amplified by circumstance. Ford narrates the father's silent piety toward his son's body (the piety we usually reserve for animals) and his effort to keep Paul's mind afloat. The epilogue is among the book's best pages: Bascombe's farewell shines with emotional common sense. "What is life better at than clinging on?"

## Key Claims

- Central question: how does a man of nearly eighty assess the happiness of his life under the American paradigm of self-improvement?
- Frank's atone prose: naming everything (brands, roads, shops) — related to Updike but without his sensual delirium.
- Cold stoicism as the dominant mode, making rare emotional peaks sharper.
- Road trip with ALS-sick son as plot: the father's silent bodily piety.
- Paul's character is amplified in mystery by his disease — circumstance occludes judgment.
- Translator: Damià Alou; publisher: Anagrama.
