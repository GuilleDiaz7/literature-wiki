# Source: Torné, "La ciudad y sus muros inciertos, de Murakami" (2024)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'La ciudad y sus muros inciertos', de Haruki Murakami: una novela que no resta ni desborda nada." *El Periódico*, 15 April 2024.

Reviewed work: Haruki Murakami, *La ciudad y sus muros inciertos* (*La ciudad y sus muros inciertos*). Trans. Juan Francisco González Sánchez. Tusquets, 576 pp. 22,90 €.

## Summary

Torné opens by asking how to review a Murakami novel — a name occupying a very visible and lightly populated space at the confluence of literature and pop culture. His eternal Nobel nomination without result is "gracious" precisely because the name is universally known but floats far from the great novelists of his time and simultaneously above the battalion of entertainment writers. Murakami is a formula novelist — flawlessly reliable in applying the formula, implacable in serving the promised rations, a professional who never allows a deviation or breaks his compact with readers. *La ciudad y sus muros inciertos* expands a shorter, older story by the author, using the same game board (and rules) to tell other stories — simultaneously a new and an old Murakami. Plot: a nameless adolescent couple falls in love; the girl speaks mysteriously of a walled city where people's "true selves" are contained by white magic. When she disappears, the boy embarks on a journey to an impossible city. Torné notes that the fan will find exactly what they expect: passages of oneiric softness, fluid prose, a symbolic game that pressures but never reveals itself, the nervous sensibility of adolescence, intermittent magic. Murakami invokes Kafka, but Torné (reading from a Western cultural area) remits him to "magical realism" — "much more sophisticated than Isabel Allende's colourful fantasies, but far below the verbal splendors of Gabriel García Márquez." The novel sits in intermediate spaces: too imprecise, immature, repetitive, and predictable to be taken seriously; too plastic, imaginative, fluid, and attractive to be mistaken for mass entertainment prose. It neither adds to nor subtracts from Murakami's fame. A feast for his followers; boring for those already exhausted by a prior title.

## Key Claims

- Murakami is a formula novelist: reliable, never deviating; between literary fiction and pop culture.
- The novel simultaneously extends and repeats an earlier story.
- Torné places Murakami within a hierarchy of magical realism: Allende < Murakami < García Márquez.
- The novel is neither serious literature nor entertaining mass fiction — permanently intermediate.
- No critical convert will be produced; no faithful reader will defect.
- Translator: Juan Francisco González Sánchez; publisher: Tusquets.

> [!contradiction] Torné's magical realism hierarchy (used for both Murakami and Rushdie) places García Márquez as the gold standard. This is a strong interpretive position — one that privileges verbal splendour as the criterion for magical realism's success. No counter-reading yet in the wiki.
