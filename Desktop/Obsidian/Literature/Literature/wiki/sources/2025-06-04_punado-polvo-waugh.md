## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews Evelyn Waugh's *Un puñado de polvo*, describing a return to Waugh's world of decadent aristocracy and the discordant notes emitted when its castles and hunting days encounter the industrial, urban, popular society expanding around them. He notes that with the passage of years, English aristocracy has declined very slowly while the surrounding society has accelerated enormously — meaning some passages read today like anthropological studies of an Amazon tribe. He offers as an example the "extravagant arrangement" by which the cuckolded protagonist Tony Last must simulate an adultery in a hotel with a stranger, paying detectives to certify it, in order to obtain a divorce without "staining the reputation" of his actually adulterous wife.

Torné characterises Waugh's writing at this stage as "decisively refractory to the literary" — refusing description of environments, psychological and emotional state examination, narrative transition. Instead: bare, austere dialogue. Pure nerve, at the price of all the juiciness associated with English prose fiction, including that of Waugh's own admired Dickens. The result resembles — in terms most recognisable to contemporary readers — the skeleton of a television script. Torné allows the style "moistens" occasionally, as if to remind the reader (and himself) that the spareness is deliberate.

Despite this style, the novel achieves four notable effects: (1) stripping the book of sentiment and proximity, persuading the reader they are watching a forensic examination; (2) transmitting Waugh's "deep discouragement and something of moral repugnance" toward his material; (3) coating even the most dramatic passages (including a child's death) with the apathetic cynicism of the aristocratic class; and (4) covering the pages with a tone that is neither parodic nor satirical but belongs to "that turbid extreme where humour is irreversibly mixed with bitterness."

## Key Claims

- Waugh's world is the decadent aristocracy in collision with modernity; time has accelerated the gap until some passages read anthropologically.
- *Un puñado de polvo* practises a style deliberately "refractory to the literary": bare dialogue, no transitions, no introspection — compared to a TV script skeleton.
- This anti-literary style achieves four specific effects: forensic detachment, moral repugnance, aristocratic cynicism over drama, and a tone mixing humour with bitterness.
- Waugh sacrificed the juiciness of English prose (including Dickens, his own model) for speed and nerve.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné's framing of Waugh's style as a deliberate formal choice (anti-literary aesthetics) is an interpretive position — others have read Waugh's dialogism as social comedy rather than moral apathy. Torné's reading of the novel's tone as mixing humour with bitterness is more convergent with standard Waugh criticism, but the emphasis on moral repugnance is distinctly his own.
