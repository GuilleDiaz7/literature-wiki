# Source: "Proust, novela familiar, de Laure Murat: la salvación"

URL: https://www.elperiodico.com/es/ocio-y-cultura/libros/20250512/laure-murat-libro-proust-novela-familiar-critica-117953989

Edition reviewed: Murat, Laure. *Proust, novela familiar*. Trans. María Teresa Gallego and Amaya García Gallego. Anagrama, 288 pp., €19.90.

## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews Laure Murat's *Proust, novela familiar*, noting that despite everything already said about Proust, commentary on him seems inexhaustible — and that Murat's approach is "particularly original and suggestive." The book proceeds from a peculiar position: Murat is the repudiated daughter of an aristocratic family whose surnames resonate throughout Proust's *Recherche*, and whose family castle has a presence in the book. She uses Marcel Proust to narrate the history of her own family — or more precisely, a collection of family memories that serve as vehicles for examining the psychology and morality of a class that has lost political influence and economic potential, but continues to fascinate in life and fiction.

Torné notes that Murat adds nothing new about the aristocracy's codes: she exposes the interminable series of norms and formalities, emptied of content, that convert these lives into a performance sustained by strict emotional containment and an aguerrida maintenance of social distances. But she says it in a different and valuable tone: that of familiarity.

The danger the book faces — and navigates — is falling into the same aristocratic trap Murat is pointing at: a display of names and dates that is itself empty. The danger is overcome by incarnating aristocratic virtues and limitations in a vivid narrative of her beloved father and embittered mother, and by pages of "precise and penetrating writing" — notably those about a storm over the castle — and pervasive sharp observations about Proust's work.

The nucleus is Murat's series of vital decisions — working, being feminist, falling in love with another woman — that made her "unassumable" for her family. Another writer might load this material with reproaches and drama; Murat tells it with the serene gesture she learned from her family, but adds "touches of deep understanding and tenderness that give the measure of the woman she has become by her own merits."

The review closes as an invitation: Murat urges reading Proust at any moment, from anywhere, despite his reputation for being snobbish and precious, because — Torné endorses — "there is no more punk writer (dissolving all social distinction) and cruel (wise, at the service of our lucidity)." And as a testament: "Proust saved my life." Torné argues the book's merit is that, reading it, one believes her.

## Key Claims

- Murat's approach is original: she uses Proust to explore the psychology and morality of her own aristocratic family.
- The book navigates — successfully — the risk of reproducing the aristocratic emptiness it critiques.
- The nucleus is Murat's series of decisions that made her unassumable for her family — told with serenity and tenderness, not reproach.
- Torné endorses the "Proust saved my life" claim as genuinely convincing rather than sentimental.
- Proust characterised as the most punk writer (dissolving social distinctions) and cruel (wise, lucidity-serving).

## Limitations or Biases

Torné shares Murat's high valuation of Proust, which colours the review. The characterisation of Proust as "punk" is a deliberately provocative reframing aimed at dislodging the image of Proust as precious and snobbish — it reflects Torné's own interpretive position. Critics approaching Proust as a monument of high bourgeois culture might contest this reading.
