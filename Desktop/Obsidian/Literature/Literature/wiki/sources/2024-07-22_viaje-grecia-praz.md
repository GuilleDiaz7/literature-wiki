# Source: Torné, "Viaje a Grecia, de Mario Praz" (2024)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Viaje a Grecia', de Mario Praz: una vía por desarrollar." *El Periódico*, 22 July 2024.

Reviewed work: Mario Praz, *Viaje a Grecia*. Trans. José Ramón Monreal. Elba, 112 pp. 20 €.

## Summary

Greece occupies an ambiguous place in Western culture: undisputed primacy in art, poetry, philosophy, and history, but centuries under Eastern domination, its populations often disconnected (by historical vicissitudes) from their glorious past. Praz (Rome, 1896–1982) arrives in Greece in 1931 — before the country's modernization — and observes the painful contrast between ancient glory and contemporary poverty with a gaze of some "sufficiency," much irony (compassionate toward the poor, implacable toward the new American rich), and a painful lucidity about what the Greeks have received in exchange for their exposed glories. He defines his own humanism austerely: its basis is the shared classical education that permits mutual recognition across cultural divides ("in the name of those classics, he and I could feel ourselves of the same civilization"). Praz presents himself as a kind of Mozart of erudition: acutely sensitive to forms, colors, textures, birdsong, and geographical accidents. His diverse strategies — mocking the rushed reconstructions of Knossos, profiling Rome's orography, acting as comic hotel and gastronomy critic, emotionally moved by a vase or statue shard, investing a literary reference with reverence — produce irresistible readability. His most outstanding quality: the precision and beauty of his natural descriptions, worthy of inclusion in an anthology of Romantic writers who loved Greece.

## Key Claims

- Praz's gaze: ironic (implacable on the nouveau riche, compassionate on the poor) and humanist (classical education as shared civilization).
- His erudition is prodigious but wearable: a "Mozart of erudition."
- The precision of his natural descriptions places him in the Romantic travel tradition.
- The visit (1931) captures Greece at a moment of maximum contrast between ancient prestige and modern misery.
- Translator: José Ramón Monreal; publisher: Elba.
