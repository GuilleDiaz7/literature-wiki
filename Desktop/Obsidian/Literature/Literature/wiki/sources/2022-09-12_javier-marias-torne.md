## Summary

Torné opens by mocking the eulogistic excess that follows a novelist's death. He refuses to claim Marías was "the best in his generation" or "the best in the world" — such claims are uncheckable and reduce novelists to racehorses. He allows that Marías's novels are superior in ambition and achievement to those of Almudena Grandes or Muñoz Molina, but insists it cannot be demonstrated that they surpass Álvaro Pombo's. The essay then turns to the substantive question: what made Marías extraordinary? The answer is twofold. First, his *sentence*: long, rich in clauses and qualifications, its sustained breathing and architectural clarity closer to James or Proust than to Faulkner's abrupt inspired bursts. Marías's distinctive feature is the obsessive, measured use of disjunctive and adversative particles and specifying clauses — this syntax allows every event to open multiple possibilities, so that narrative "reality" is attenuated, subject to competing versions that compete not only in truth-value but in melodic attraction and narrative interest. His narrators are not idiots or liars but insecure voices that entangle the reader in hypothesis, making direct access to events difficult. Second, his *temporal management*, which he learned from translating Sterne: his protagonists distance themselves from events through hyper-slow narration, reasoning about them sometimes before understanding what is happening — in these gaps open contemplative periods (film commentary, childhood memories, anticipations, philological digressions, jokes or witticisms). The best of Marías's emotional temperature comes from these long runs of novelistic thought, pursuing a topic's various aspects without exhausting or concluding it.

## Key Claims

- The death-occasion genre (best of his generation, etc.) is dismissed as uncheckable.
- Marías's primary achievement: the syntactic sentence with disjunctive particles — reality attenuated by competing versions.
- Narrators: not unreliable in the traditional sense, but insecure — entangling the reader in hypothesis.
- Temporal management: learned from Sterne; hyper-slow narration opens contemplative gaps.
- The best Marías is in the long contemplative run of thought, not in plot events.
- Comparisons: closer to James and Proust than Faulkner.
