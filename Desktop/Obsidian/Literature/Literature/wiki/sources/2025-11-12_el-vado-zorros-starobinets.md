# Source: "El Vado de los Zorros, de Anna Starobinets: iconos"

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'El Vado de los Zorros', de Anna Starobinets: iconos." *El Periódico*, 12 November 2025.
URL: https://www.elperiodico.com/es/ocio-y-cultura/libros/20251112/anna-starobinets-libro-vado-zorros-critica-123303582

Edition reviewed: Starobinets, Anna. *El Vado de los Zorros*. Trans. Viktoria Leftérova and Enrique Maldonado. Impedimenta, 776 pp., €34.95.

## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) opens by placing Anna Starobinets within the horror genre — dominant in the world of film and television and growing in literary prestige — and contextualising her epithet "the Russian queen of horror." He then addresses the critical category most often applied to her: magical realism. His argument is definitional: magical realism requires a realistic framework as its dominant mode; without this, the works of Mary Shelley, Bram Stoker, Poe, and Stephen King would have to be classified as magical realists, which makes no sense — the magical in those works is at the heart of the narrative, not an incursion into realism.

What Torné finds instead in *El Vado de los Zorros* is "a sort of exotic and eccentric historicism." Exotic because the novel is set in a remote zone of China; eccentric because, though the action occurs during the Second World War, Starobinets's interest is not military, social, or economic but rather in a crossing of mystical and marvellous elements: fox-women, mysterious monks, perverse gods, werewolves, mad scientists in search of the ultimate weapon. The guide through this "heterogeneous nightmare landscape" is Maxim Cronin, a circus artist who has escaped a concentration camp.

Given these elements and "the pumping of strange and dark novelties that does not stop for 700 pages and the diverse narrative techniques that succeed each other," Torné argues that Starobinets's poetics is one of *apabullamiento* — of overwhelming. Her closest literary relative, he suggests, is "a Thomas Pynchon who takes the eccentricities of his characters quite seriously" — though without Pynchon's wide-ranging critique of market narratives and military capitalism. The review ends on the enigma of Maxim's fate, leaving the question open.

## Key Claims

- The label "magical realism" misapplies to Starobinets: her framework is not realist but fantastic from the start; the correct category is "exotic and eccentric historicism."
- The novel's dominant poetics is *apabullamiento* — an overwhelming accumulation of strange elements and narrative techniques over 700 pages.
- Closest comparandum: Pynchon — serious about characters' eccentricities but without Pynchon's political-critical ambition.
- The review does not resolve its own central question about the novel's purpose or meaning.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné's definitional argument about magical realism is his own position — it is contestable within the broader critical debate about the genre's boundaries. His framing of the 700-page novel as overwhelming without pronouncing a clear evaluative verdict leaves the reader somewhat in the dark. The review may be less negative than it appears — "apabullamiento" could be read as impressed as well as resistant.
