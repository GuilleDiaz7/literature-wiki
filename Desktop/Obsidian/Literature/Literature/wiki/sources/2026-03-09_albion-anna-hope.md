## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews Anna Hope's novel *Albión* (published in Spanish translation by Libros del Asteroide, 400 pp., €24.95; Catalan edition: Àmsterdam, 464 pp.), translated by Regina López Muñoz and Esther Roig i Casamitjana. Torné argues that the novel suffers from a fundamental conceptual error: it mistakes the rural setting common to classic English fiction for a genre in itself, producing what he calls "literatura decorativa" — ornamental fiction that offers a nostalgic tour through a literary tradition that never really existed.

The novel follows a family gathering at an imposing Sussex estate after the death of patriarch Philip Ignatius Brooke. The cast includes a stoic and cuckolded wife, three adult children (the responsible one, the rebellious one, the sex addict), and a mysterious figure from the past who accuses the Brookes of founding their fortune on the slave trade. Torné sees this premise — an English aristocrat discovering his wealth derives from predatory exploitation — as naively conceived: conservatism, he suggests, is precisely the ideology of returning to an age of unaccountable abuses.

Torné's main critical claim is that Hope has misread the tradition she draws on. Jane Austen's country novels were not really about country houses; *Emma* is about resistance to others' narratives of us; *Mansfield Park* about the negotiation between gratitude and rebellion; *Persuasion* about the persistence of bad decisions made to please others. The rural setting was a backdrop — as the city was for Dickens, the sea for Conrad. Later novelists who used rural England (Forster, Murdoch) were doing so to examine mature literary themes, not to reconstruct a decorative atmosphere.

Torné concedes that Hope is a technically competent writer who knows her target reader and delivers consistently from the first page. But technical competence without the ironic nerve of Austen or the dark passion of Murdoch amounts to a "leve falsificación" — a mild falsification — of the tradition being invoked.

## Key Claims

- *Albión* mistakes setting for genre: the "English country house novel" is a retrospective construct inflated by film and TV adaptations, not a coherent literary tradition.
- Austen, Dickens, Conrad, Forster, and Murdoch used rural/urban/maritime settings as backdrop for substantive literary and moral investigations; Hope uses setting as content.
- Hope's prose craft is competent (dialogue, characterisation, description, dramatic revelations) but lacks the ironic intelligence of her models.
- The slave-trade revelation subplot is critically dismissed as conceptually naive.
- The novel is ideal reading for those seeking a pleasant, undemanding experience — but constitutes a "downgrade" of the literature it takes as its model.
- The danger of decorative fiction is that, by invoking great literature without its confrontational nerve, it produces gentle falsification.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné writes as a literary critic with pronounced aesthetic commitments to formal and intellectual seriousness in fiction. His framework privileges irony, moral complexity, and confrontation of difficult ideas over pleasurable, accessible reading. The charge of "decorativeness" reflects this position and may not be shared by critics sympathetic to genre fiction or popular literary entertainment. The review does not engage with Hope's stated intentions or with any prior critical reception of the novel.
