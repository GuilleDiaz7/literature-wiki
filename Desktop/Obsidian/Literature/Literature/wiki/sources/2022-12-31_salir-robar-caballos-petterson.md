# Source: Torné, "Salir a robar caballos, de Petterson" (2022)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Salir a robar caballos', de Per Petterson: miradores del tiempo." *El Periódico*, 31 December 2022.

Reviewed work: Per Petterson, *Salir a robar caballos* (*Ut og stjæle hester*). Trans. Cristina Gómez Baggethun. Libros del Asteroide, 272 pp. 19,95 €.

## Summary

Torné opens with an unusually strong claim: Petterson is one of the very few active novelists with a personal and achieved literary project — well above, in ambition and talent, Houellebecq, Cărtărescu, and Franzen. His novels function on two levels: readable individually, or as chapters in a single future book that would enclose the whole corpus, revising and extending the exposure of his characters' lives from multiple social and temporal angles. *Salir a robar caballos* is Petterson's most independent novel and risks being confused for what it is not: the title is "slightly mellow," the premise is well-worn (Trond, near retirement, retreats to an isolated cabin), the temporal alternation is clichéd. But from these clichés a different novel emerges. Petterson's evident talents: description of natural landscapes, construction of mundane voices, everyday emotionality, much physical labor (scything, clearing snow, felling trees). Subtler talents: the contrast between the muted palette of the present and the vivid resplendence of the past; very audacious ellipses. His interest lies not in psychological progress but in the accumulation of experience that converts the protagonist into a different witness of the same episode. The novel operates on a double temporal desajuste: the young Trond cannot understand what he is experiencing as well as the reader; the reader cannot understand the emotional weight of certain passages until many pages later. Petterson's central insight: time is not a continuous advance that never returns, but a geometric form allowing one to see events from different angles depending on one's position. This produces some of the most moving and unexpected scenes in contemporary literature.

## Key Claims

- Petterson ranks above Houellebecq, Cărtărescu, and Franzen — an unusually strong comparative judgment.
- Time as geometric form (not linear flow) is Petterson's defining formal-philosophical contribution.
- The double desajuste (young Trond can't understand / reader can't understand until later) is the novel's structural achievement.
- The clichéd surface (cabin, alternating temporal planes) conceals a formally inventive novel.
- Translator: Cristina Gómez Baggethun; publisher: Libros del Asteroide.
