## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews the reissue of John Fowles's *El Mago*, framing it as a classic that "belongs more to readers than to critics." He opens with a series of examples of the reader-critic gap: critics may see Salinger's Fitzgerald debts or find Cortázar's geometric tricks impatient — but readers return to both. *El Mago* is similar: a critic might note the rough narrative voice, mechanical dialogues, imperfectly incorporated erudite material, and a somewhat artificial intrigue that loses interest on rereading — "the critic's touchstone: to intuit whether the text will resist a second reading." None of this has dulled the pleasure of thousands of readers drawn by rhythm, narrative cunning, and the morbid attraction of dark magic.

Torné has "serious doubts that *El Mago* is a masterpiece" but is certain that readers who enter its game will enjoy the journey: the story of a harsh orphan from Oxford who sails blindly to a Greek island and becomes the victim (or beneficiary) of an "eccentric millionaire's" psychological games.

The review's main critical contribution is a careful treatment of the term "postmodern" as applied to the novel. Torné distinguishes a legitimate use of the term in England (as a chronological marker for novels after Joyce's and Woolf's modernism — experiments with time, the primacy of language over plot, verbal explorations of consciousness) from the more loaded philosophical sense of postmodernity (the philosophical recognition that no guarantees exist in nature or the subject for discovering truths unconditioned by situation or interest). His argument: the novel already does what this second sense of postmodernism claims to inaugurate — every character's statements are situated, conditioned by temperament and circumstance, countervailed by other characters' "truths." This is not postmodern novelty but the novel's essential condition since Cervantes's *Quixote*.

Torné also develops the novel's exploration of the "figure of negative charisma" — the sage, the eccentric millionaire, the politician who offers to exchange freedom for security, the voice that whispers "give me your will." He situates this in the tradition of Thomas Mann's *Fausto* and Iris Murdoch's novels about charismatic male figures. The reissue serves, he argues, for readers to reflect on "the dangers of abandoning oneself to the temptation of a will that presents itself as superior."

## Key Claims

- *El Mago* is a classic that belongs to readers more than critics — formally imperfect but irresistibly pleasurable.
- The "postmodern" label applied to Fowles is imprecise; the novel's perspectivism is not philosophically postmodern but the basic condition of fiction since Cervantes.
- The figure of negative charisma — the will that presents itself as superior — is the novel's central moral theme; this places it in the tradition of Mann's Fausto and Murdoch's charismatic male figures.
- The reissue serves as a meditation on the dangers of surrendering one's will to an apparently superior authority.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné's argument about "postmodernism" reflects his consistently skeptical relationship with theoretical labelling — he deflates the term by showing it describes what fiction always already did. This is a rhetorical strategy as much as a theoretical argument. Scholars working within postmodern literary theory might defend Fowles's postmodernism on more specific grounds (metafiction, self-referentiality, the novel's multiple drafted endings) that Torné does not address.
