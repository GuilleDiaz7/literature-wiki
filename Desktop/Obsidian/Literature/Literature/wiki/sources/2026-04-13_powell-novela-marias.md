Note: Originally the prologue to *Juego de espera*, by Michael Powell. Barcelona: Reino de Redonda, November 2019.

Reviewed work: Michael Powell, *Juego de espera* (*A Waiting Game*, 1975). Trans. not specified. Reino de Redonda.

## Summary

Marías begins by correcting the received assumption about the Powell-Pressburger partnership (The Archers, 1941–1959): that Pressburger was the writer and Powell the visual craftsman. In fact, Powell directed films before and after the collaboration, while Pressburger's solo work (novels, guionista credits) was largely negligible. The assumption stems from the difficulty of understanding how two-person creative collaborations actually work — we imagine division of labor that may not exist. Powell revealed his literary gifts in two massive volumes of memoirs (*A Life in Movies*, 1986; *Million-Dollar Movie*, 1992) and, less known, in *A Waiting Game* (1975), his only novel — which he never mentioned in either memoir. The novel's subject is Ireland in 1952 — the Donohue family, farmers and hunters, carrying the weight of civil war memory ("the only real war is civil war... between neighbors and citizens of the same country... friend against friend, brother against brother") and its consequences for the present. The patriarchal figure voices this memory with chilling nostalgia. The novel is narratively very well constructed, full of surprises; it cannot be reduced to a non-realized screenplay — its narrative information structure is impossible to translate directly to the screen. Marías rates it as good in its domain as Powell's greatest films, which he lists in full (from *The Edge of the World* to *Age of Consent*). Miguel Marías is identified as a Spanish film critic.

## Key Claims

- The Powell-Pressburger division of labor (Pressburger writes, Powell directs) is a received myth, not a documented reality.
- *A Waiting Game* (1975) is Powell's only novel, unmentioned in either volume of his memoirs.
- Subject: Ireland 1952, civil war memory and its lasting poison.
- The novel is narratively complex and resistant to screen adaptation — not a screenplay in disguise.
- Marías rates it as high as Powell's best films.
- Fifth critic in the wiki: Miguel Marías (Spanish film critic).
