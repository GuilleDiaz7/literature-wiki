# Source: Torné, "El movimiento del cuerpo a través del espacio, de Shriver" (2023)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'El movimiento del cuerpo a través del espacio', de Lionel Shriver: más gimnasia, menos gimnasia." *El Periódico*, 15 July 2023.

Reviewed work: Lionel Shriver, *El movimiento del cuerpo a través del espacio* (*The Motion of the Body Through Space*). [Publisher not specified in source. Trans. not specified.]

## Summary

Torné frames the novel as a satirical retablo of contemporary customs: a married couple — Remington Alabaster (sedentary man who, having lost his job to a feminist boss, pivots to marathon-training obsession) and Serenata Terpsichore (formerly a dedicated athlete who develops arthrosis at exactly the moment her husband discovers exercise) — exchange their bodily regimes just as they turn sixty. The novel produces comic situations via agile prose and sharp dialogue. The satire targets the cult of physical fitness and body-worship as secular religion. Torné values the book's wit and intelligence but situates it below the standard set by *Tenemos que hablar de Kevin*, Shriver's first and most devastating novel. This book is "malévolos y lúcido como divertido" — malevolent and lucid and funny — but with less edge, in a more relaxed tone. Torné's verdict: a competent satirical novel that adds a further panel to a career permanently overshadowed by *Kevin*.

## Key Claims

- *Kevin* casts a long shadow; everything Shriver writes is read against it.
- The body-worship cult is targeted as a secular religion — satire is the book's dominant mode.
- Agile prose, sharp dialogue — the comedy is the main achievement.
- Verdict: enjoyable but secondary to *Kevin*; less sharp, more relaxed.
- Novel names are onomastically malicious (Remington Alabaster, Serenata Terpsichore).
