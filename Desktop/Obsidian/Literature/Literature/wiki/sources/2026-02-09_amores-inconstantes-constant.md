# Source: "Los amores inconstantes, de Benjamin Constant: intermitencias"

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Los amores inconstantes', de Benjamin Constant: intermitencias." *El Periódico*, 9 February 2026.
URL: https://www.elperiodico.com/es/ocio-y-cultura/libros/20260209/benjamin-constant-critica-libro-amores-inconstantes-126359272

Edition reviewed: Constant, Benjamin. *Los amores inconstantes*. Trans. Manuel Arranz. Periférica, 328 pp., €21.90.

## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews the collected edition of Benjamin Constant's four *nouvelles* — *Adolphe*, *Cécile*, *El cuaderno rojo* (*Le Cahier rouge*), and *Amélie et Germaine* — published together by Periférica under the collective title *Los amores inconstantes*. Torné calls the volume "one of the literary events of the year" and a "literary feast."

The review opens with a methodological caution: Constant's *nouvelles* are habitually read within a biographical framework (one is presented as a biographical notebook; another relates to two of Constant's actual love affairs). Torné argues this is an impoverishing lens. The biographical origin of material is irrelevant compared to the "alchemic process" by which the novelist transforms lived experience into fiction — a process that leaves no traces and returns us to the texts alone. Ten novelists working from the same experiences would produce ten distinct novels.

Torné proposes an alternative frame: the French literary tradition inaugurated by Blaise Pascal's insight that "the heart has reasons reason does not know" — what he calls the tradition of *las intermitencias del corazón* (the heart's intermittencies). This tradition runs from Pascal through Constant to Honoré de Balzac and Marcel Proust. Constant's contribution is a rigorous, unsparing study of the discrepancies between what is good for us and the secret motives of the heart — motives that frequently operate against our own interests, dignity, and happiness.

Torné distinguishes Constant from the two great successors of this tradition. Unlike Balzac, Constant dispenses with vast social canvases and relational networks. Unlike Proust, he forgoes broad temporal arcs where thoughts about a subject evolve and qualify over decades. Instead, Constant concentrates all resources on one couple (or two) in a time-span stripped of density: the result is an *aphoristic* writing style in which each page contains a valuable literary thought. The few nature observations shine like rare metals; the sparse metaphors are placed to release illumination at exactly the right moment. These are chamber works (*obras de cámara*).

The review briefly characterises each *nouvelle*: *El cuaderno rojo* as delightful sketches of youth in a frivolous intimacy Stendhal might have signed; *Amélie y Germaine* as a contrapuntal description of two women through a bitter and misogynist consciousness; *Amélie* as a portrait of a narrator who perceives the love that suits him with perfect clarity while resolved to take other paths.

## Key Claims

- The biographical reading of Constant is a "impoverishing framework" that mistakes origin for essence in fiction.
- Constant belongs to the French tradition of the heart's intermittencies: Pascal → Constant → Balzac → Proust.
- Constant's distinguishing formal feature is concentrated, aphoristic prose — chamber works rather than the large social frescoes of Balzac or the temporal panoramas of Proust.
- Each *nouvelle* has a distinct tone despite all sharing a recognisable family resemblance.
- The collection as a whole is one of the literary events of 2026 in Torné's assessment.

## Quotations of Significance

> "La de Constant es una escritura aforística, donde cada página contiene un pensamiento literario valioso, donde brillan las escasas observaciones sobre la naturaleza como metales raros, y las contadas metáforas están situadas para soltar su carga de iluminación en el momento preciso."

## Limitations or Biases

Torné argues programmatically against biographical criticism. Scholars in the biographical or genetic criticism traditions would likely contest his dismissal of the autobiographical frame as impoverishing. The review does not engage with the French tradition of autobiography (*autofiction*) or with recent scholarship on Constant's manuscripts and their relation to the published texts. Torné's evaluation of the collection as a major literary event of the year is an aesthetic judgment, not a scholarly claim.
