# Source: Torné, "Bruja Luna, Rey Araña, de Marlon James" (2023)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Bruja Luna, Rey Araña', de Marlon James: ¿fantasy de luxe?" *El Periódico*, 31 December 2023.

Reviewed work: Marlon James, *Bruja Luna, Rey Araña* (*Moon Witch, Spider King*). [Publisher/translator not specified in source.]

## Summary

Torné previously read *Black Leopard, Red Wolf*, the preceding entry in James's series mixing fantasy with African myths and the anxious intermittences of the unconscious. He found it a confusing mosaic. *Bruja Luna, Rey Araña* is an improvement: it focuses on a more charismatic protagonist (the Moon Witch, a sorceress of almost two hundred years who narrates how she failed in the first novel and her battle against Aesi, a figure of tyranny). This perspective provides a narrative conduit and reduces the previous book's sprawl. However, core problems persist: James employs symbolism that is too transparent (tyrannical power, the woman who rebels through witchcraft) and his frequent violence is extreme, flat, like an adolescent tantrum. The novel's core failure is James's indecision: does he play the fantasy genre straight, or use it for more complex literary purposes? He is not, Torné says, an Ishiguro capable of turning science fiction into a devastating political novel (*Cuando fuimos huérfanos*). His attempts to elevate adventure material through passages of "grand style" and disturbing visions rarely rise above decorative. The closing metaphor: James tries to fly but cannot lift his prose off the ground, like birds that have wings but have lost the ability to sustain themselves in the air (ostrich, emu, penguin).

## Key Claims

- Improvement over first volume: more cohesive, better protagonist.
- Fatal flaw: indecision between generic genre fiction and literary ambition.
- Symbolism is too transparent; violence lacks depth.
- Not comparable to Ishiguro's use of genre for literary ends.
- The prose "wants to fly but cannot take off."
