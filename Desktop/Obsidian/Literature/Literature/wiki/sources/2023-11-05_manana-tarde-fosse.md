# Source: Torné, "Mañana y tarde, de Jon Fosse" (2023)

## Bibliographic Details

Torné, Gonzalo. "'Mañana y tarde', el último libro del premio Nobel Jon Fosse publicado en España." *El Periódico*, 5 November 2023.

Reviewed work: Jon Fosse, *Mañana y tarde* (*Morgon og kveld*). Trans. Cristina Gómez-Baggethun and Kirsti Baggethun. Nórdica / De Conatus, 112 pp. 18 €.

## Summary

Written on the occasion of Fosse receiving the Nobel Prize (2023), Torné's review is explicitly a partial reading — a "dipping of toes into the sea." Fosse (Haugesund, Norway, 1959) is better known in France than in Spain, and better known as a dramatist than as a novelist. The review focuses solely on this brief text. Fosse's style: built on repetitions, a syntactic disposition that may remind the Spanish reader of Bernhard, though Fosse is less concentrated and obsessive, and may have in mind the enveloping litanies of the Bible. Verbal and wordplay effects serve a slightly disheveled poetry; the language never quite dances. But the combination offers a prose very sensitive to special states: the gaze of a woman who has just given birth, the first hearings of a newborn, the weight of everyday tools charged with experience. Structure: the novel shows the first and last day of the same man's life (Johannes, a fisherman). More precisely: the hours before birth (when the baby's body breathes empty of consciousness) and the hours after death, without it being clear whether we see supernatural transcendence or the last agitations of a subjectivity. Fosse does not withhold the revelation that Johannes is dead — he counts on readers recognizing it quickly. What matters is following the fisherman's bewilderment, his gentle anguish at intuiting, without acknowledging, that he is dead. A comparison is offered: *Fresas salvajes* (but Torné cites Angela Thirkell here, seemingly confused with Bergman), Dickens, Japanese ghost literature. The book's greatest virtue: it dares to speak of the everyday mysteries of existence in a language felt and sensitive, stripped of intellectual sophistication — the words of a fisherman, of a man Christ could have chosen to walk beside him.

## Key Claims

- Style: Bernhard-like repetitions but less obsessive; litanic quality perhaps Biblical in origin.
- Structure: first and last day of a life — before birth and after death.
- The fisherman's post-death bewilderment is the novel's emotional core.
- Greatest virtue: speaking of everyday mysteries without intellectual sophistication.
- Torné notes that this brief text cannot represent Fosse's work as a whole (primarily a dramatist).
- Translators: Cristina Gómez-Baggethun and Kirsti Baggethun; publishers: Nórdica / De Conatus.
