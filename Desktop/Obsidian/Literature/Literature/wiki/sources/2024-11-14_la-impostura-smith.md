## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews Zadie Smith's *La impostura*, her debut in the Victorian historical novel — a sub-genre Torné frames as almost a rite of passage for contemporary British writers. He cites Ian McEwan's *Expiación*, Julian Barnes's *Arthur y George*, and the Victorian opening of Kazuo Ishiguro's *Nunca me abandones* as precedents, arguing that setting a novel in the Victorian era has become a recognisable genre unto itself, allowing contemporary novelists to work with shared literary references while inscribing their own contemporary techniques and purposes.

Smith uses the double plot — a device beloved by Thackeray, Dickens, and George Eliot — as her structural principle. The novel's protagonist, Eliza Touchet, drives a plot that interweaves a minor Victorian literary figure (with the ego of a thoroughbred) and a legal imbroglio where nobody is who they seem. The title's impostura (imposture) floats across characters throughout the book — a "dancing signifier" that invites the reader to continuously reorganise the meaning of the material.

Torné identifies two distinctively contemporary choices. First, the novel's scale: though as long as a Victorian volume, it is constructed from very short pieces — almost gags or informational pills — compressed by humour, separated by ellipses, very evasive. Second, its symbolic dimension: the events are spread across time and so carefully selected and arranged that they demand interpretive keys beyond the action itself.

His critical reservation is also double: the condensation into brief narrative sequences becomes repetitive, and when documentary material is introduced, even lazy. He judges that there is a mismatch between the materials deployed, the technique, and the aims of the book — and that the proposal prolongs itself over pages and "closes somewhat falsely." He stops short of saying the novel is too long, which he explicitly calls "a pretentious licence for any critic facing a writer of this stature."

## Key Claims

- *La impostura* participates in a recognisable contemporary British sub-genre: the Victorian historical novel as rite of passage.
- Two contemporary formal choices: construction from very short units (almost viñetas); and a symbolic dimension requiring interpretive keys beyond the plot.
- The title's imposture functions as a floating signifier, applicable to multiple characters and situations.
- Critical reservation: mismatch between materials, technique, and purpose; the brief-sequence approach becomes repetitive; the novel closes somewhat falsely.
- Torné declines to call the novel too long but implies it.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné holds Victorian fiction to a high standard as the benchmark against which contemporary Victorian pastiches are measured. His critique of the brief-unit structure reflects his general preference for sustained prose fiction over fragmentary or episodic construction.
