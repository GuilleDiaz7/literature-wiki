## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews *Radio Benjamin*, an illustrated collection of radio broadcasts Walter Benjamin made for children around 1930, when he was "fascinated with a new toy: the radio." The broadcasts adopt a fluid but not unusual form — halfway between chronicle and story — informative, suggestive, "with a tone of surprise and adventure, as if meant to be read by the fire on a dark night: witches, earthquakes, highwaymen... Emotional traps to fascinate youthful emotions."

Torné notes that Benjamin simplifies his syntax, attenuates his prophetic faculties, and entirely abandons learned erudition — but never adapts his topics to an Adamic vision of childhood, nor hastens his narratives toward moralising conclusions. Instead, "he treats the 'youngest ones' as a less trained species of adults who deserve respect." The broadcasts have interest for adults too: to hear Benjamin in an amicable, didactic tone — "deploying the charms of the 'storyteller' to which he dedicated so many inspired pages" — and to notice how he manages to filter always-suggestive reflections on moral questions (evil, delinquency, repression, catastrophe) that in his characteristic style are "always interwoven with politics and social dynamics."

The review's second half, which Torné says would be incomplete without it, focuses on Judy Kaufmann's illustrations. He poses the question: can a philosophical universe be "translated into images"? Referencing Eisenstein's conviction that one could make a film of Marx, Torné argues that Kaufmann demonstrates it is possible to "draw" the thinking contained in these radio stories. Her illustrations are varied but tend toward the iconic — often silhouettes in black, almost always supported by a texture, with "the will to present flat images as condensations of ideas, in the way that on a road the silhouette of a deer warns of its presence."

More significant still, Torné argues, is Kaufmann's mode of linking icons into page or double-page illustrations — arrangements that recall mosaic tesserae, strange architectural constructions, or the deployment of a dream experience: "a suggestive simultaneity that challenges the more progressive character of diurnal consciousness." This poetic reflects key tics of Benjamin's own thinking: "his reticence to linear argumentation, his distrust of conclusions, and also his taste for the aphorism, the parable, and the enigmatic fulguration of an intelligence very close and somewhat strange, closer the stranger it is."

## Key Claims

- Benjamin's radio broadcasts treat children as "less trained adults" — refusing both moralisation and Adamic naivety.
- The broadcasts deploy the storyteller's art Benjamin theorised elsewhere.
- Kaufmann's illustrations successfully translate Benjamin's philosophical universe into a visual language.
- Her mode: iconic silhouettes as idea-condensations; page-level compositions recalling mosaics, architecture, or dreamscapes.
- Torné's central claim: Kaufmann's visual poetics mirrors Benjamin's own intellectual tics — anti-linear, aphoristic, enigmatic, closer the stranger.

## Limitations or Biases

The review is structured around the illustrated edition and thus evaluates Kaufmann's contribution as integral rather than supplementary. Critics interested in Benjamin's texts alone might weight the review's priorities differently. Torné does not engage with Benjamin scholarship or with the texts' political-pedagogical context in Weimar Germany.
