## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews the Spanish publication of *Física de la tristeza* (*Physics of Sadness*), the second novel by Bulgarian author [Gueorgui Gospodínov](../authors/gospodinov_gueorgui.md) (Yambol, 1968). The review opens by noting — and dismissing as irrelevant — the legend that the Bulgarian first edition sold out in a single day. More importantly, Torné clarifies this is a 2011 text recovered for the Spanish market; the author appears already fully formed.

The novel's structural premise: a boy protagonist encounters a young Minotaur and, struck by the monster's sadness and alienation, develops a kind of empathic super-sensitivity — the ability to inhabit other subjectivities and transfigure emotionally. From this motor, the narration leaps across space, time, registers (real/fantastic), and kingdoms (human/animal/vegetable). The symbolic key is simple and legible: we are all trapped in labyrinths of strangeness and confusion, feeling ourselves to be more than we appear. Torné finds this narrative traction effective.

The review's main critical turn is taxonomic. Torné identifies Gospodínov's chief talent as *imagination*, but notes it is an imagination of a specific kind: not suited to character development, not precise in narrative scenes, indifferent to moral examination. The compensation is a vivid imagination for *estampas* (vignettes), paradoxical and surprising situations — bordering, Torné notes with mild apology, on magical realism. These fixed images allow emotionally precise writing from which Gospodínov extracts wit and tenderness.

The crucial evaluative distinction: the novel reads better as an *imaginary field notebook* or *diary of occurrences* than as a novel in the traditional sense. Read as a novel, the symbolic scaffolding seems simplistic and the escape from form feels underdeveloped — a missed opportunity for higher formal or thematic synthesis. Read as a cuaderno de campo imaginario, however, the book's stature grows. This formal angle — novel vs. diary — changes the meaning and quality of what is read, in the same way that a sunny day tastes different in a dry summer than as a respite after a storm.

Torné also notes a distinctive quality in Gospodínov's temporal sensibility — his pleasure in shuffling epochs and confusing personal with historical time — which makes him, unexpectedly, a character in the tradition of Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*.

## Key Claims

- *Física de la tristeza* is the work of an already fully developed author, not a novice text.
- Gospodínov's talent is imagination — vividly inventive for vignettes and paradoxical situations — but limited in character development, narrative scenes, and moral examination.
- The novel touches on magical realism in its mode of imagination.
- The book is "literatura del pellizco" — literature of pleasant pinches — offering varied emotional experiences in well-crafted scenes but not deep immersion.
- The formal frame matters: the book reads better as an imaginary field notebook than as a novel.
- Gospodínov's shuffling of epochs and fusion of personal with historical time places him in the tradition of Virginia Woolf's *Orlando*.

## Quotations of Significance

> "El rasgo más destacado del talento literario de Gospodínov es la imaginación, de una especie no muy diestra en el desarrollo de personajes, borrosa en las escenas narrativas y ajena al examen moral."

> "La de Gospodínov es una literatura del pellizco, pellizcos muy agradables, y aunque no invitan a una gran inmersión, nos ofrece una valiosa variedad de emociones en escenas muy elaboradas."

> "Pensada como novela, el andamiaje simbólico parece algo simplón, y la fuga algo endeble: desaprovecha la oportunidad de articularse en una forma o en un sentido superiores."

## Limitations or Biases

Torné values novels that pursue formal or thematic synthesis, deep character, and moral seriousness. His critique of *Física de la tristeza* is calibrated against these expectations; a critic sympathetic to lyrical, fragmentary, or autofictional forms might reach a more favourable formal assessment. The review engages only with Torné's own reading and does not situate the novel within Bulgarian literary tradition or post-communist Eastern European fiction more broadly.
