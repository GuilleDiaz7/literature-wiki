## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews Damon Galgut's *En una habitación ajena*, a book of three novellas that Torné argues, taken together, compose a novel. The three stories share tone, technique, and theme: all are autobiographical in appearance, all feature a younger Galgut observed with curiosity by an older narrator who remembers facts but not motivations, and all are desolating.

Galgut's consistent style across the three is analytical, with lyrical crests in descriptions, a vague sensitivity to social differences, touches of reflection on travel literature, and constant attention to the moral profile drawn by the stories' impact on the narrator-protagonist — a figure who overlaps or identifies with the author's younger self.

Torné analyses each novella in turn. The first recounts the narrator's attempted approach to an attractive young German; they embark on a trip together and the narrative becomes a power struggle played out in small gestures — "a vignette of domination and psychological cruelty in the vein of Musil, but without the master's precision." Torné faults it for narrative predictability and clichés about amorous obsession. The second — which he judges the best — follows the narrator across Africa and Switzerland pursuing a young man he can barely communicate with: "a fantasy about suspended lives, the value and danger of hunches, and the complications of filling an attractive body with an invented character." The ending is predictable but the journey earns it. The third, and weakest in Torné's reading, follows the narrator accompanying a self-destructive friend on a recuperation trip to India; the story descends into "melodramatic excess of effective disasters," and the narrator's final-page tears "shine like a crocodile's."

The book's overall thesis, Torné concludes, is not about travel but about displacement — not going far away but being out of place, "protagonising a life we don't recognise as our own." He ends with the observation that reviving the travel literature genre is very difficult.

## Key Claims

- The three novellas form a novel through their shared tone, technique, and theme.
- Galgut's style is analytical with lyrical crests — attentive to social difference and the moral impact of experience on an autobiographical protagonist.
- The first novella: predictable, Musil-adjacent but without Musil's precision; burdened by clichés of amorous obsession.
- The second novella: the best; a fantasy about suspended lives and the danger of hunches.
- The third novella: weakest; collapses into melodramatic excess.
- The book's true subject is displacement rather than travel — living a life not recognised as one's own.
- Torné doubts travel literature can be revived as a genre.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné's negative assessment of the first and third novellas is based largely on criteria of originality and narrative predictability. The charge of "clichés about amorous obsession" may reflect his general scepticism toward psychologically intimate first-person narration. No other critical readings of this book are in the wiki.
