## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews *La estatua*, an inédito (previously unpublished work) by Günter Grass, which he characterises as the master's "farewell to literature" — albeit cautioning that such designations should be handled with care. The book is a miniature that pays homage to paper: "that space where, as is repeated several times, everything is possible."

The narrative proceeds as a curious tracking shot: it begins with an invitation Grass and his wife receive to cross the Berlin Wall and read passages from their books; continues with Grass imagining a dinner with medieval figures; then inviting the founding fathers of Germany to dinner on paper, though what arrives are not the founders themselves but the ordinary people who served as models for their statues. Among these is a search for the young woman who modelled for the statue of Uta von Naumburg — considered by Umberto Eco the most fascinating woman of the Middle Ages.

At this point, Torné notes, one must concede with Grass that when everything is possible on paper, what matters is determining with some precision what one is trying to achieve. For the most interesting aspect of *La estatua* is precisely "the skill with which Grass decides to withhold most of what is offered to us." Through its leaps between reality and history, history and fantasy, fantasy to story-imagination, the text blurs whether Grass is declaring love to the changing faculties of literature, recording the nostalgic persistence of eroticism in old age, or purging a concrete passion (behind a cascade of coloured literary veils) for a real woman.

Torné reads the book's serene atmosphere curdling into directed remorse — directed, one supposes, at Grass's wife Ute, who shares her name with the Uta of the statue. The sustained ambiguity sends Torné to compare the narrator with the governess of *The Turn of the Screw* — a Victorian innocent unable to see clearly. He concludes by inviting readers to lose themselves in this "apparatous and fascinating labyrinth in miniature that emits from death he who in life was a prophet of all excesses."

## Key Claims

- *La estatua* is a miniature valediction to the power of paper and imagination.
- Its structure is a cascade: reality → history → fantasy → story, with no stable ground.
- The key critical observation: Grass deliberately withholds what he is doing — the message is encoded behind literary veils.
- The central ambiguity: is it a declaration of love to literature, a record of late-life eroticism, or a coded confession of a real passion?
- Torné implies the book encodes a real relationship, obscured behind the Uta/Ute parallel.

## Limitations or Biases

The review is unusually speculative: Torné reads the book as a personally coded text about a real relationship, but this interpretation is inferred from internal patterns (Uta/Ute) rather than documented evidence. Torné acknowledges the master's reasons remain his own. As a posthumous work, the absence of authorial commentary makes all readings provisional.
