URL: https://www.elperiodico.com/es/ocio-y-cultura/libros/20250406/critica-libro-desfile-rachel-cusk-modelos-sabotaje-115876788

Edition reviewed: Cusk, Rachel. *Desfile*. Trans. Catalina Martínez Muñoz. Libros del Asteroide, 184 pp., €18.95.

## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) reviews Rachel Cusk's *Desfile*, identifying its subject as the sabotages and self-sabotages of feminine creation from a multiplicity of viewpoints — social, political, anthropological, maternal, ideological, conjugal, artistic, unconscious, customary, racial — which Cusk makes parade from first to last page. The book progresses "unconcerned rather than indecisive" about whether it belongs to the novel or non-fiction; Torné notes that when the pages are good, genre classification is irrelevant.

The structure is elaborate: four chapters, each with two alternating narrative segments that seem (without certainty) to alternate fiction and testimony, and to vary in tense, space, gender, and historical period. All are united by a family of similar preoccupations and by a series of artists known only by the initial "G" — their name's or surname's first letter — in what Torné calls "a nominal Orlando" that mutates throughout the book, referencing Virginia Woolf's shape-shifting protagonist.

Torné identifies two major talents. The first: Cusk's approach to art. Rather than describing artworks in words (a common novelistic error, "wasting plastic potency and suggestion by confining it in atonal paragraphs"), she uses artistic descriptions as "launchers for the study of animated and vital situations, frequently violent, bypassing the need for a narrative scaffolding for her ideas. The images lead us to ideas."

The second, and more intense, talent is her exploration of emotional registers — generally feminine but by proximity also masculine — that are "convincing and original." Torné quotes Emily Dickinson as a comparandum for Cusk's "cognitive originality": she only activates when she has something concerning but barely expressed to deploy. Her style is dry, analytical, distant, and ironic, yet — "despite its almost glacial staging" — transmits tenderness through its closeness to lived experience and its attention to emotional tones we had overlooked.

Torné's closing characterisation is a list of paired paradoxes: "Always original and never extravagant, always mundane and never costumbrista, always firm but never inflexible, ambiguous in the measure that it strives to extract layers hidden by commonplaces." The result is a text of "very peculiar density" that invites savouring in sips.

## Key Claims

- *Desfile* explores the sabotage of feminine creation from every angle simultaneously.
- Its formal structure — four chapters, alternating fiction/testimony, floating "G" character — constitutes a "nominal Orlando."
- Cusk's first great talent: using art descriptions as emotional-situational launchers rather than word-paintings.
- Cusk's second great talent: cognitive originality — she only writes when she has something barely expressed to offer; style dry and ironic but tender through closeness to lived experience.
- Torné's framework: Emily Dickinson as comparandum for Cusk's cognitive originality.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné's praise of *Desfile* is among the most unqualified in the current wiki corpus. His comparison with Emily Dickinson for cognitive originality is evaluatively very high. The review does not engage with feminist critical theory directly, treating Cusk's subject matter as content to be assessed formally. Critics who approach Cusk from a feminist theory or autofiction framework might produce significantly different analyses.
