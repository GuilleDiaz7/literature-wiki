## Summary

[Gonzalo Torné](../critics/torne_gonzalo.md) opens by reflecting on Bernhard's arrival in the Spanish literary landscape — introduced through a first mention by Javier Marías ("the rhythm of the torrent will always be demented") — and the subsequent "Bernhard virus" that spread among Spanish writers. He notes that Bernhard, like Borges, is nearly impossible to imitate without falling into parody. He then makes a notable critical claim: "perhaps the only writer whose prose benefited from the virus was his most constant translator: [Miguel Sáenz](../translators/saenz_miguel.md)."

He situates *Andar* precisely within Bernhard's career: a "transitional space" after the three great masterworks (*Trastorno*, *La calera*, *Corrección*) and before the best chamber works (*El malogrado*, *Maestros antiguos*), which it in some sense preludes. The novel's argument fits, as so many Bernhard novels do, in a "comic strip panel": two friends walk and recall a third who has been admitted to a sanatorium after a nervous breakdown while buying trousers. Bernhard proposes a superficial similarity between walking and thinking, but "it becomes immediately apparent that thinking is much more complex than innocent walking."

Bernhard's prose is described as a "sadistic device" — an echo chamber where voices reflect others' words (often memories of a third party), sensitive to paranoid and delirious nuances applied to banal topics (the fabric of a pair of trousers), parallel absurdities, sudden pronouncements, and games with prepositions and italicised emphases that leave the reader "somewhat bewildered."

From this disorientation, Bernhard launches his favourite subjects: "punishing the procreation of children, the world as a madhouse, the genius against the State, circumstances and chance, that the most sensitive people are the least considered, the sinister relative, the incompetence of psychiatrists, the impossibility of living in the country or the city, neither in one's own country nor abroad." And through all this, the book's crucial subject: "the monstrousness of something as everyday as thinking" — of the intellectual efforts we make with faculties as weak as memory, of the daily sustaining of a consciousness that feeds, parasite-like, on our energy.

## Key Claims

- *Andar* occupies a transitional position in Bernhard's oeuvre: post-masterworks, pre-chamber works.
- The plot is comic-strip simple; the formal complexity lies in Bernhard's "sadistic" prose device — echo chamber, paranoid nuances, preposition games.
- Notable claim about translation: Miguel Sáenz is the only writer whose prose genuinely benefited from the Bernhard virus — a positive assessment of translation influence.
- The novel's crucial subject: the monstrousness of everyday thinking.
- The bewildered state the prose induces in the reader is the intended effect, not a side effect.

## Limitations or Biases

Torné's review operates on the assumption that Bernhard's style is a known quantity to the implied reader, and the opening on the "virus" metaphor already assumes a shared familiarity with this critical discourse about Bernhard's Spanish influence. The evaluation of Miguel Sáenz as the only writer to genuinely benefit is a strong claim that would be contested by other translators and Bernhard scholars. The review's enthusiasm for the work as an entry point into Bernhard is itself a form of advocacy.
